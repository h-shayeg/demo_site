Why Most Molecular Property Predictors Fail to Generalize

By Kian Rostami·January 2026·6 min read

On this page

  • Introduction
  • The Benchmark Illusion
  • Distribution Shift, Not Model Capacity
  • What Scaffold Splitting Reveals
  • Conclusion

Introduction

A model that performs well on a benchmark split often collapses on genuinely novel scaffolds. The culprit is usually distribution shift, not model capacity — and conflating the two leads to a lot of wasted effort tuning architectures that were never the actual problem.

The Benchmark Illusion

Most public molecular property benchmarks report a single accuracy number under a random train/test split. That number is real, but it answers a narrower question than it looks like it answers: how well does this model interpolate within a chemical space it has already seen substantial parts of? It says very little about extrapolation to structurally novel compounds — which is usually the setting anyone doing real discovery work actually cares about.

Distribution Shift, Not Model Capacity

When a model's validation accuracy is high under random split and much lower under scaffold split, the instinct is often to reach for a bigger model or a fancier architecture. That rarely helps, because the problem isn't capacity — it's that the model has learned features correlated with the training distribution's specific scaffolds rather than the underlying structure-property relationship. A larger model with the same evaluation blind spot just memorizes more efficiently.

Generalization Gap = Acc(random split) − Acc(scaffold split)

A large gap indicates scaffold memorization rather than transferable learning.

What Scaffold Splitting Reveals

Scaffold splitting groups molecules by their core structure — typically the Bemis-Murcko scaffold — and ensures the same scaffold never appears in both train and test sets. It's a stricter, more honest evaluation, and it's also less flattering, which is probably why it's still not the default in a lot of published benchmarks.

A model's accuracy under random split tells you how well it memorized. Its accuracy under scaffold split tells you how well it learned.

Conclusion

None of this means benchmarks are useless — they're a necessary starting point. But treating a random-split accuracy number as the final word on a model's real-world usefulness is a mistake I've made myself, more than once. Report both splits, and be suspicious of any result that only reports one. It's the evaluation protocol built into MolGraphNet by default, for exactly this reason.
