Research

Research

My research asks how machine learning can be built on top of chemical structure, rather than around it — using molecular graphs, physical priors, and reproducible pipelines to make computational chemistry both more predictive and more trustworthy.

Research Areas

01

Machine Learning

Molecular Property Prediction

Most cheminformatics pipelines still represent molecules as fixed-length fingerprints, discarding the very structure that determines chemical behavior. My work uses graph neural networks that operate directly on the molecular graph — atoms as nodes, bonds as edges — to predict properties such as aqueous solubility, reaction yield, and toxicity. The goal isn't marginal benchmark gains; it's building models that generalize to genuinely novel scaffolds, which is where most published methods quietly fail.

Related project: MolGraphNet 2 related publications

02

Machine Learning

Generative Molecular & Materials Design

Screening existing chemical libraries only gets you so far — the more interesting question is what molecules should exist that don't yet. I work on generative models, primarily variational and diffusion-based approaches, that propose candidate structures conditioned on target properties. The hard part is less the generative model itself and more making sure proposed candidates are synthesizable and chemically valid, not merely optimized on paper.

Related project: LatentMol 1 related publication

03

Software

Reproducible & Scalable Scientific Software

A model is only as useful as the pipeline that produced it, and most computational chemistry pipelines are effectively unreproducible eighteen months after publication — undocumented preprocessing, dependencies pinned nowhere, one-off scripts. I build open-source, configuration-driven tooling that treats a research pipeline as software: versioned, tested, and runnable by someone who wasn't in the room when it was built.

Related project: ChemPipe 1 related publication
