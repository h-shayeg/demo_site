Neural Reaction Predictor

A deep learning model that predicts chemical reaction yields from molecular descriptors — built to cut days of blind screening down to hours.

Role

Lead developer & researcher

Timeline

Jan – Jun 2025

Stack

PyTorch, RDKit, FastAPI-style service

View on GitHub Live demo

Overview

High-throughput reaction screening in the lab is slow: every candidate reaction has to be run, worked up, and analyzed before you know whether it was worth trying. This project trains a model on historical reaction data to predict yield ahead of time, so the wet lab only runs the reactions worth running.

The problem

Our team was screening roughly 40 reaction conditions per week by hand, with a hit rate — reactions above 60% yield — of under 15%. That meant most bench time went to reactions that were never going to work. We needed a way to rank candidates before committing reagents and instrument time.

Approach

I built a graph neural network that takes molecular structures as input (via RDKit-generated graph representations) and predicts expected yield along with a confidence interval. The model was trained on a combination of public reaction datasets and our own lab's historical records, then fine-tuned as new results came in.

  • Featurized reactants and reagents as molecular graphs
  • Trained a message-passing neural network in PyTorch
  • Wrapped the model in a small internal API so it could be queried from the lab's existing dashboard
  • Added an active-learning loop so new experimental results improve the model over time

Results

After three months of use, the lab's hit rate on screened reactions rose from 15% to 46%, and the average number of physical trials needed to find a viable condition dropped by more than half. The model is now a standard step before any new reaction screen.

"It's changed how we plan a week of bench work — we screen with the model first and only run what it's confident about."

What I'd do differently

The active-learning loop took longer to get right than expected, mostly around avoiding feedback bias toward reaction types we already had lots of data on. Next time, I'd design the sampling strategy for that from day one rather than retrofitting it.
