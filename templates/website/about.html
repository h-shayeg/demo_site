Biography

Biography

I didn't start out planning to work in machine learning. My background is in chemistry — specifically, the kind of physical organic chemistry that involves a lot of time in front of a fume hood, and even more time afterward trying to make sense of the data it produced. Somewhere in my second year of undergraduate research, I started writing small Python scripts to automate the tedious parts of that analysis, and the scripts kept getting more interesting than the experiments.

That interest turned into a PhD in computational chemistry, where I spent most of my time asking a fairly narrow question: can a model that understands molecular structure — not just a list of numerical descriptors — predict chemical behavior more reliably than the fingerprint-based methods most of the field still relies on? The graph neural network library that came out of that work, MolGraphNet, ended up being used well beyond my own thesis, which taught me almost as much about software engineering as the research itself did.

Today I split my time between research — mostly generative models and property prediction — and maintaining the open-source tools that research depends on. I care a lot about the second half of that sentence. A result that can't be reproduced by someone outside the original lab isn't really a scientific result yet, just a claim, and a fair amount of my day-to-day work is really about closing that gap.

Research Philosophy

Research Philosophy

Chemistry doesn't need machine learning to be more mysterious. It needs machine learning to be more honest about what it doesn't know.

It's easy to build a model that fits a benchmark. It's much harder to build one that behaves reasonably when it's wrong — and in chemistry, being wrong has consequences a leaderboard doesn't capture. I try to treat uncertainty, reproducibility, and interpretability as first-class parts of the research, not as an afterthought added for the camera-ready version.
